## Edition timing (the decisive check)

Unlike 1936 (which had a separately cataloged `-02` "EXTRA EXTRA" edition)
and 1940 (which had an editorial boasting of a rushed ~6 a.m. special),
**no evidence of a rushed/special edition was found for 1944**:

- No second CDNC-cataloged edition exists for this date under `NCT` — every
  search phrase tried (`the`, `San Diego county`, `precincts`, `evening
  except`, `extra edition`, `o'clock`) returned hits only under the single
  `NCT19441108` issue, never a `-02`-suffixed sibling like 1936's.
- **Routine schedule confirmed, unchanged**: page-2 masthead
  (`NCT19441108.1.2`, zoom-verified at
  `mirror/cdnc/screenshots/nct1944_p2_full.png`) reads "Published every
  evening except Sunday and legal holidays, by PERCY EVANS, at 7 East
  Grand Avenue, Escondido, California" — same routine EVENING schedule as
  1934/1936/1940.
- **No "extra edition" self-reference anywhere in the issue** (unlike
  1936's explicit "TIMES-ADVOCATE OFFICE GIVES ELECTION SERVICE" item and
  1940's "scooped the universe" editorial) — `search:"extra edition"` = 0
  hits.
- **No clock phrase (a.m./p.m./"this morning"/"tonight") attached to the
  county returns box itself.** The "LATEST REPORTS IN S. D. COUNTY ON MAIN
  ISSUES" box (below) carries only a "By United Press" byline and no
  dateline/timestamp.
- **Circumstantial evidence points to a late-Wednesday (not pre-dawn)
  compilation**: the same page 1 carries a wire story "3rd Army Strikes To
  Flank Stubborn Enemy Stronghold... PARIS, Nov. 9. Lt. Gen. George S.
  Patton's American Third army made a new attack..." — a dispatch datelined
  the DAY AFTER this issue's own Nov. 8 masthead date. Since Paris ran many
  hours ahead of Pacific War Time, a Paris "Nov. 9" dateline corresponds to
  Wednesday afternoon/evening Pacific time at the earliest — meaning this
  issue's wire content was still being assembled well into Wednesday, not
  finalized before dawn.
- The paper's own editorial column (page 2, "AND WE QUOTE") reflects
  retrospectively on the concession: "THAT WAS certainly a grand speech that
  Dewey made at 12:15 election night. (We can praise his speeches now.)" —
  a reflective, already-know-the-outcome tone consistent with routine daytime
  composition, not a rushed pre-dawn dispatch.

**Verdict on timing: UNCONFIRMED as night-clocked; the balance of evidence
(routine evening masthead, no special-edition marker, no explicit clock
phrase, a same-issue dispatch datelined a day later) points to a
same-day/afternoon compilation.** Per RUNBOOK 5.2 and the precedent set in
`1932-11-08.md` and the `1934-11-06.md` addendum, this is recorded as a
CEILING, not a night count.

## ADDENDUM (2026-07-12, special-edition hunt): NO SPECIAL EXISTS — the CEILING STANDS, and is now clock-proven

Second agent pass, tasked specifically with hunting a SPECIAL/EXTRA edition that
could upgrade the body's ceiling to a night count (the 1936 `-02` / 1940
"scooped the universe" fact patterns). **No special edition exists for this
election.** The ceiling stands. Two new lines of evidence, both stronger than
anything in the body above:

### 1. A structural, method-validated proof that no `-02` extra exists (either paper)

The body ruled out a `-02` sibling by *phrase searching* — an argument from
silence, and the body itself flagged that as non-exhaustive (human-verification
ask #2). That ask is now **CLOSED** by a positive structural test.

CDNC's month-calendar page (`?a=cl&cl=CL2.<YYYY>.<MM>&sp=<CODE>`) renders each
day's link one of two ways, and the difference is diagnostic:

- a date with **exactly one** edition → a direct issue link, `a=d&d=<CODE><date>`
- a date with **more than one** edition → a day-level *list* link,
  `a=cl&cl=CL2.<YYYY>.<MM>.<DD>` (you must click through to pick an edition)

**Control (proves the test detects a known extra):** NCT's November **1936**
calendar renders every day as a direct `a=d` link *except one* —
`cl=CL2.1936.11.04` — which is exactly the date of the confirmed
`NCT19361104-02` "EXTRA EXTRA" edition. The signature works.

**Applied to 1944:**

| Paper | Nov 1944 issues found | Multi-edition days in Nov 1944 |
|---|---|---|
| `NCT` (Escondido Times-Advocate) | Nov 1,2,3,4,6,7,8,9,10,11,13,...,30 (25 issues) | **NONE** — every day is a direct `a=d` link, including Nov 7 (election day) and Nov 8 |
| `BT` (Oceanside Blade-Tribune) | Nov 1,2,3,6,7,8,9,10,13,...,30 (21 issues) | **NONE** |

Retrieval: `https://cdnc.ucr.edu/?a=cl&cl=CL2.1944.11&sp=NCT&e=-------en--20--1--txt-txIN--------`
(and `&sp=BT`), saved to `/Users/sbuss/.claude/jobs/8f24b289/tmp/sd/nct_nov1944_cal.html`
and `bt_nov1944_cal.html`; 1936 control saved to `nct_nov1936_cal.html`.

So: **neither San Diego County daily printed a second (extra/special) edition on
any day of November 1944** — not on election day, not on the day after. There is
no night edition to find. The election-day issues (`NCT19441107` / `BT19441107`)
are single, routine editions that went to press before polls closed and
therefore cannot carry returns at all.

### 2. The Blade-Tribune supplies the clock phrase NCT withheld — and it says "afternoon"

The body's timing verdict was "UNCONFIRMED... balance of evidence" because
NCT's own county box (`NCT19441108.1.1`, "LATEST REPORTS IN S. D. COUNTY ON MAIN
ISSUES," byline "By United Press") carries **no dateline or timestamp**. The
Oceanside Blade-Tribune's day-after issue prints **the same United Press county
compilation, at the same precinct basis, and does timestamp it.**

- Issue: `BT19441108`, Oceanside Daily Blade-Tribune, masthead "SAN DIEGO
  COUNTY, CALIFORNIA — WEDNESDAY, NOVEMBER 8, 1944 — VOL. XVII, NO. 220"
  (banner: "4th TERM FOR ROOSEVELT"). Routine edition type: **EVENING**
  ("Published Every Evening Except Sundays and Holidays," established from this
  paper's own masthead in `1932-11-08.md`).
- Section `BT19441108.1.1`, article "STREAM, DRIVER IN CLOSE RACE FOR ASSEMBLY;
  IZAC RE-ELECTED; F. D. R., DOWNEY, GET HEAVY VOTE". OCR:
  `mirror/cdnc/BT19441108/search_precincts.txt` (query `search:precincts`, 1 hit).
  Vision-read digit-legible at `mirror/cdnc/screenshots/bt1944_county_crop.png`
  (crop of `bt1944_county_zoom2.png`; full page `bt1944_p1_full.png`):

> "**Late county returns secured from the United Press by the Blade-Tribune at
> 3:30 o'clock this afternoon** indicates a close race between Charles Stream and
> Robert Driver for the state assembly. Stream had 12,202 and Driver 12,181 in
> 116 precincts out of 223.
> Ed Izac was way ahead of James Abbey in the contest for United States
> congressman. Izac had 64,243 to Abbey's 49,757 in 596 precincts out of 752.
> Fred Houser was trailing Sheridan Downey in the county vote by over four
> thousand votes. Downey had 59,425 and Houser 55,353 in 596 precincts out of 752.
> **The county gave President Roosevelt a comfortable majority in a vote of
> 66,474 to Thomas Dewey's 52,494 in 596 precincts out of 752. Watson had 665
> votes.**"

The presidential figures (66,474 / 52,494) and the precinct basis (596/752) are
**digit-identical to NCT's untimestamped box**, and the Senate row matches too
(Downey 59,425 / Houser 55,353). It is demonstrably the same UP county
compilation. The Blade-Tribune states when it received it: **3:30 p.m. Wednesday,
November 8** — roughly **19.5 hours after polls closed** at 8 p.m. Tuesday.

**This converts the body's "UNCONFIRMED" timing into a positive, quoted
disproof.** The 596/752 figure is not a night count and never was: it is an
afternoon-of-the-day-after count, the exact fact pattern of the corrected
`1932-11-08.md`. The ceiling verdict is not merely unrefuted; it is now proven.

(Incidental corroboration of the count's slowness, same page, same issue: "Many
of the precinct officials worked into the wee hours of the morning completing
their tabulation.")

### 3. The ceiling number is REVISED UPWARD: the body missed a candidate

California's 1944 presidential ballot carried **three** tickets, confirmed by the
Blade-Tribune's own "Local Election Returns" table on the same page ("Roosevelt
and Truman" / "Dewey and Bricker" / "**Watson and Johnson**" — Claude A. Watson,
Prohibition). NCT's county box printed only Roosevelt and Dewey, so the body's
sum of 118,968 is an incomplete contest sum. The Blade-Tribune prints the third
candidate at the same 596/752 basis: **Watson 665**.

Per the wave's rule (sum ALL candidates in the single-seat contest), the correct
ceiling at this basis is:

| Candidate | Votes |
|---|---|
| Franklin D. Roosevelt (D) | 66,474 |
| Thomas E. Dewey (R) | 52,494 |
| Claude A. Watson (Prohibition) | 665 |
| **Contest sum (CEILING)** | **119,633** |

**Ceiling = 119,633 ballots**, 596 of 752 precincts (79.3%), clocked 3:30 p.m.
Wednesday 1944-11-08 by the Blade-Tribune. (The body's 118,968 supersedes to
119,633; the difference is Watson's 665.)

### Revised verdict

**CEILING (confirmed, not a night count).** Ceiling = **119,633 ballots**
(Roosevelt 66,474 + Dewey 52,494 + Watson 665) at 596/752 precincts (79.3%),
timestamped 3:30 p.m. Wednesday 1944-11-08. Share: **NULL**, `comparable: false`
— because the figure is afternoon-clocked (a ceiling can never carry a share).
The denominator is no longer pending: the certified San Diego County President
contest sum is **166,764** (SOV 1944 pp.4-6, `denominators.md`), so the ceiling
is bounded at **<=71.7%** of certified. The true election-night
plateau for San Diego County in 1944 is **unknown and strictly below 119,633**
(i.e. strictly below 71.7% of certified),
and no surviving digitized source can recover it: the county's only two
digitized dailies are both evening papers, and neither printed an extra.

**1944 is a documented dead end for a night count.** Closing note for the
campaign: the 1940 win was not repeatable in 1944 because the Times-Advocate's
overnight-special behavior was a one-off staff stunt (their own 1940 editorial
says the editor was *surprised* by it), not a standing practice.
